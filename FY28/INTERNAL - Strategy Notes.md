# RSU 5 Engagement Notes

**March 2026** | Internal reference for community engagement preparation

---

## Purpose

These are working notes for preparing to engage in the RSU 5 restructuring conversation — at Board meetings, in community forums, and in conversations with district leadership. The goal throughout is constructive participation grounded in data.

The public-facing community engagement proposal (District Strategic Planning Council, evaluation criteria, timeline) is now in the omnibus document (Part VII). These notes cover personal preparation: what talking points to lead with, what principles to follow, and how to keep the conversation productive.

---

## Engagement Principles

1. **Lead with data, match the tone.** District communications have been warm, reasonable, and values-driven. Combative or adversarial responses will alienate the Board and the broader community. The strongest approach is to agree with stated values — thoughtful planning, sustainability, listening — while populating them with auditable data.
2. **Respect the people doing the work.** The Superintendent and Board members are professionals navigating a genuinely difficult situation. The disagreement is about specific proposals and their financial consequences, not about character or intentions.
3. **Acknowledge the hard truths.** PES does cost more per student than the district average ($26K vs. $23.2K). Pownal does have a modest consumption deficit ($267K). These facts are real and should be stated honestly before explaining why they don't justify closure. Credibility comes from acknowledging both sides.
4. **Make the affirmative case, not just the defensive one.** "Don't close PES" is a defensive message. "Here is a restructuring plan that funds the EC mandate, saves $479K–$804K, preserves all three schools, and is fully reversible" is an affirmative one.
5. **Present this as all three communities working together.** This is not Pownal vs. the district. All three towns are affected by restructuring decisions — Freeport and Durham families face changes under every scenario too.

## Key Talking Points

These are the core data points to bring into public conversations. All are sourced in the omnibus.

**Staffing:** Independent DOE data shows PES is the leanest-staffed school in RSU 5 — it suffered the largest staffing decline of any school over the past nine years (-28.4%), more than double the next largest (FHS: -12.4%). Four of six schools lost FTE; central office grew +13.3%. The cost premium at PES is not from overstaffing; it's from irreducible fixed costs spread over 105 students instead of 274.

**Financial analysis of Scenario 2:** The full Scenario 2 restructuring saves only 2.05 teachers beyond what middle school consolidation alone achieves. After $600K in new transportation and $74K in facility conversion, the analysis shows a net annual cost of ~$161,000 — not savings. Risk-adjusted: -$560K to -$650K/year.

**The alternative:** Path A consolidates middle grades for efficiency, distributes EC services to home communities, preserves all three schools, and produces estimated savings of $479K–$804K. Combined with revenue alternatives, the total offset is $1.3M–$1.9M.

**Equity:** Pownal taxpayers pay $21,699 per student in local taxes — nearly double Durham's $11,034. The cost-sharing formula adds $382K/year above what a per-student split would yield.

**Enrollment:** PES is the only elementary with consistent growth: up from 89 (FY24) to 105 (FY27 projected). MSS (-42) and MLS (-17) are declining.

**Independence analysis:** Financial modeling shows Pownal independence could save ~$169K–$339K/year after a SpEd risk reserve at FY28 projected rates. Withdrawal under §1466 is the only mechanism that definitively protects PES as a K-5 school. Every other protection — §1512 (reconfiguration loophole), reorganization plan amendments (cannot complete before the withdrawal deadline and require Board cooperation), interlocal agreements (provide a right to sue but not power to prevent conversion), private and special laws (2026 legislative session effectively closed) — either cannot be completed within the timeline where PES is at risk or cannot prevent conversion if the Board decides to proceed.

## Two-Track Framing

Withdrawal is the path for protecting PES. Advocacy for RSU 5 reform is a parallel track driven by genuine concern for all three communities. These are not in tension:

**Why withdrawal is primary.** The legal analysis (omnibus Section 6.10, PES Protection Legal Research) examined every available mechanism for protecting PES within RSU 5. None can both (a) be completed within the timeline where PES is at risk and (b) reliably prevent conversion if the Board decides to proceed. An interlocal agreement — the only negotiated protection that fits the withdrawal timeline — gives Pownal a cause of action (the right to sue) but not a prevention mechanism. If the Board breaches it, by the time courts resolve the dispute, PES would already be converted. Specific performance (a court order forcing the Board to maintain K-5 instruction) is rarely granted against governmental bodies. The most likely remedy would be damages — money, not a reconstituted school. Withdrawal resolves the protection question definitively: Pownal owns PES.

**Why reform advocacy continues regardless.** Pownal's relationship with Durham and Freeport does not end with withdrawal. Approximately 100 Pownal students will continue attending FMS and FHS under tuition agreements — a multi-decade relationship. Community organizations (Tri-Town Little League, Freeport United Soccer, Freeport Community Services, RSU 5 Community Programs) cross town lines and exist independently of RSU governance. The systemic issues documented in the omnibus — cost-sharing formula deadlock (Section 5.6), the broken budget feedback loop (Section 7.3), the 17-year cycle of conflict (Section 7.4) — affect Durham and Freeport too. Pownal advocates for fixing them because they matter for those communities, not because Pownal needs them as an alternative to withdrawal.

**What an independent Pownal would implement.** The same governance principles the omnibus advocates for within RSU 5 — transparent budgeting, structured community engagement, shared data, evaluation criteria for decisions — would be implemented in Pownal's own district governance. The proposed Strategic Planning Council structure (Section 7.5) works equally well for a 200-student independent SAU, arguably better given the smaller and more responsive governance structure.

**The message.** Pownal is pursuing withdrawal because it is the only reliable way to protect PES. Pownal is also advocating for RSU 5 reform because the systemic issues are real and affect everyone. These tracks reinforce each other. If the Board produces structural reforms during the withdrawal process, those reforms benefit Durham and Freeport regardless of what happens with Pownal. If withdrawal completes, Pownal continues as a partner community through tuition, shared activities, and geographic proximity — not an adversary.

## Gray Presentation: Key Framing Gaps (March 2026)

The Superintendent's "Planning for the Future of RSU 5" (February 11, 2026; first made public via Pownal Select Board agenda, March 23, 2026) provides several useful rhetorical entry points. Full analysis: `INTERNAL - Gray Presentation Analysis.md`.

**Use the presentation's own values language.** The presentation identifies "community identity" as one of four primary decision lenses and states: "Each school is an expression of community identity and history." Scenario 2's own community impact section acknowledges "significant change to Pownal identity, loss of K-5 neighborhood school." This is the district's own framing arguing against its own proposal.

**Use the "hard to predict" admission.** The presentation states: "Financial implications are always hard to predict precisely." This was used to excuse the absence of any financial modeling. Turn it: "We agree that financial implications are hard to predict. That's exactly why we built a model. The numbers show a net cost, not savings."

**Use the "right vs. right" framing constructively.** The presentation closes with "A true dilemma is never right vs. wrong, it is always right vs. right." Agree with the premise, then fill it with substance: "We agree this is a values question. Our values include not making irreversible decisions without financial modeling, waiting for the $100K facilities study the Board just approved, and treating the unified voice of a community as meaningful input."

**The engagement contradiction.** The presentation advocates for community engagement as a primary consideration while being presented to a town whose unified input has not altered a Board outcome in 17 years. This is documented factually in the Omnibus (Sections 7.3, 7.4, 8.1-8.4). For internal use: "The presentation asks how we should engage all communities. We have been engaging. What has changed as a result?" For public use: let the historical record speak for itself.

**Scenario 3 is not a third option; it is a second way to close PES.** Both Scenario 2 and Scenario 3 displace Pownal K-5 students to DCS. The specific repurposing of PES is secondary to whether Pownal loses its school at all. Our Path A achieves the same middle school consolidation benefits without closing any school.

**Select Board letter (pending March 23 approval).** The Town is formally contesting the "repurposing" characterization and initiating FOAA requests for withdrawal data. This establishes that the withdrawal evaluation is a town-level action, not solely a parent advocacy effort.

## Conversation Reminders

- Start by affirming shared goals. Everyone wants sustainable, high-quality education for all three communities.
- Focus on three data points most people haven't seen: the DOE staffing trend, the true net cost of Scenario 2, and the Path A alternative.
- When discussing withdrawal, frame it as a practical necessity for PES protection, not a rejection of the communities. Pownal values the relationship with Durham and Freeport and expects to be partners for decades through tuition and community organizations.
- When discussing RSU 5 reform, frame it as genuinely important for all three towns — not as a bargaining position. The systemic issues are real regardless of whether Pownal stays or goes.
- Don't ask anyone to commit to anything in the moment. Share the data and offer it as a resource.
- Offer to contribute the analysis to a shared data foundation. The goal is a process where conclusions emerge from shared analysis.

## Notes on Tone

The analysis reaches specific conclusions (e.g., Path B produces a net cost, not savings). These should be stated as findings with citations, not as opinions. "The analysis indicates..." and "the data shows..." are appropriate framings. Avoid "we believe," "we recommend," or "the Board should."

Where the data is uncertain or assumptions are debatable, say so. Credibility depends on intellectual honesty about limitations as much as confidence about findings.

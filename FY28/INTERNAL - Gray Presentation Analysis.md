# Analysis: "Planning for the Future of RSU 5"

**Superintendent Tom Gray, February 11, 2026**
**Internal working document, March 2026**

---

## Overview

On February 11, 2026, Superintendent Tom Gray presented "Planning for the Future of RSU 5" to the RSU 5 Board of Directors. The presentation was not made public at the time. It became publicly available on March 21, 2026, when it was posted as an attachment to the Pownal Select Board agenda for March 23, 2026, where Gray is scheduled to present the same material to the Select Board.

The presentation is 20 slides covering three restructuring scenarios, framed around four lenses: Early Childhood Programming, Middle School Equity, Financial Sustainability, and Community Identity. It contains no financial figures for any scenario.

A copy is archived at `data/RSU 5 Presentations/PlanningfortheFutureofRSU5.pdf`.

---

## Claims That Align With Our Analysis

These points are accurate and should be acknowledged in public discussion. Credibility requires affirming what the data supports.

**1. Budget growth pressures are real and statewide.** The presentation identifies health insurance, salary obligations, and special education as primary cost drivers. This is correct. RSU 5's proposed FY27 increase (6.53%) is within the range of peer districts (3.9% to 9.88%). RSU 5 spends $20,344/pupil vs. a state average of $19,804, only 2.7% above. (Omnibus 3.7)

**2. RSU 5 is not a spending outlier.** The presentation's framing that these pressures are structural and not unique to RSU 5 matches our analysis. MSAD 51 Cumberland ($22,054), RSU 21 Kennebunk ($23,866), and RSU 75 Topsham ($24,452) all spend more per pupil. (Omnibus 3.7)

**3. Morse Street space constraints are real.** MSS sits on a constrained downtown lot with no play fields, one playground, and multiple deferred maintenance items ($350K to $400K estimated). Maine Chapter 124 requires 75 sq ft of outdoor space per preschool child; MSS's 64 PreK students need 4,800 sq ft on a lot that struggles to provide it. (Omnibus 1.7)

**4. Community identity matters.** The presentation identifies community identity as one of four primary lenses. We agree this should be weighted heavily. The presentation's own Scenario 2 community impact section acknowledges "significant change to Pownal identity, loss of K-5 neighborhood school."

**5. Middle school equity and consistency are real concerns.** Academic offerings are inconsistent between FMS and DCS. Extracurricular and athletic equity is a legitimate issue. Our Path A addresses this directly by consolidating grades 7-8 at FMS. (Omnibus 2.2)

## Claims That Require Scrutiny or Rebuttal

### 1. "Exploratory conversation" framing

The presentation is titled as an exploratory exercise: "This is an exploratory conversation, seeking Board guidance" and "Goal: Determine which ideas to explore further." The closing asks "Which option(s) warrant deeper operational analysis?" and "How shall we engage the community?"

**The reality:** The Board unanimously committed to Cohort 3 of the EC SpEd transition on February 4, 2026, with a July 2027 deadline. District leadership has publicly stated intent to move forward with restructuring. The timeline does not support an open-ended exploration. The presentation's tone of measured deliberation does not match the pace of decisions already in motion.

### 2. EC mandate conflation

The presentation asks: "We have space for the 4-year-olds, but where do we put the 3-year-olds in 2027?" This implies a space crisis for all preschool-age children.

**The reality:** The LD 345 mandate (Title 20-A §7209-A) requires only special education services for children ages 3 to 5 with disabilities, approximately 25 students. Community PreK programs (128 students across three towns) are voluntary under Maine law. The Maine DOE Public Pre-K Guidebook (January 2025) states: "public preschool programming remains optional for any school administrative unit." The presentation never makes this distinction. Scenario 2's proposal to centralize all PreK at PES substantially exceeds what the mandate requires. (Omnibus 2.1)

### 3. Scenario 2 financial claims: no numbers

The presentation states: "Single early childhood facility vs. multiple sites," "Long-term operational efficiencies possible," "Consolidated middle school programming," "Reduced duplication of resources and staffing." No dollar figure accompanies any of these claims.

**Our analysis:** After accounting for 8 new bus routes, DCS coordination costs, and amortized PES conversion costs, Scenario 2 produces a **net annual cost of $87,000 to $161,000**. It costs money. It does not save money. The entire restructuring saves only 2.05 teachers beyond what middle school consolidation alone achieves. (Omnibus 2.3, 2.4)

### 4. "Duplicating staff and resources"

The presentation lists "duplicating staff and resources in multiple locations" as a limitation of the current configuration.

**The reality:** DOE data shows PES is the leanest-staffed school in the district. PES lost 28.4% of its FTE over 9 years, more than double the next largest decline (FHS at 12.4%). PES's share of district staff (~4.5%) is roughly half its share of students (~10.5%). The school has lost dedicated ELA specialist, Math specialist, and Teacher Support Team assignments. PES is not duplicating resources; it has been systematically thinned. (Omnibus 1.5, 3.5)

### 5. "Financial implications are always hard to predict precisely"

This statement is used in the Financial Sustainability slide as a caveat. Taken at face value, it is true. Used in this context, it serves to excuse the absence of any cost modeling from the presentation.

**The reality:** Financial modeling is precisely what should inform a decision of this magnitude. The absence of numbers in a restructuring proposal affecting three towns and six schools is itself a significant finding. Our analysis provides a full cost model with line items, source citations, and sensitivity ranges. (Omnibus 2.2, 2.3, 2.4)

### 6. Middle school equity as a Scenario 2 benefit

The presentation presents middle school consolidation (all 7-8 at FMS) as a benefit of both Scenario 2 and Scenario 3. The educational merit is real.

**The reality:** Middle school consolidation can be achieved without closing PES. Our Path A does exactly this, producing savings of $479,000 to $804,000 per year while preserving all three elementary schools. The benefit is real; coupling it to PES closure is logically unnecessary. (Omnibus 2.2)

### 7. Scenario 3: district-wide 6th grade at PES

The presentation proposes a third scenario: PES becomes a district-wide 6th grade school (~157 students), Pownal K-5 moves to DCS, all 7-8 students go to FMS.

**Key observations:**
- Pownal still loses PES as a K-5 school. Students still bus to DCS.
- Requires full staffing at PES for ALL content areas (Art, Music, World Language, FLS, SpEd, PE) for a single grade. Economy of scale is poor for 157 students in one grade.
- Does not solve the EC SpEd mandate. The presentation itself acknowledges: "Does not fully solve early childhood challenges" and "offers no solution for 3 yos."
- Our Path A achieves the same middle school integration by placing grade 6 at DCS and grades 7-8 at FMS, without removing any school from its community.
- Under both Scenario 2 and Scenario 3, Pownal loses its K-5 school. The "which scenario" question is secondary to whether Pownal should lose its school at all.

### 8. Transportation costs: not mentioned

The presentation does not reference transportation costs for any scenario. Scenario 2 requires at minimum 8 new bus routes (3 for Pownal K-6 to DCS, 3 for Freeport PreK to PES, 2 for Durham PreK to PES), estimated at $600,000/year. Scenario 3 requires transporting all district 6th graders (~157) to PES plus all Pownal K-5 (~105) to DCS. These are not minor line items. (Omnibus 2.3)

### 9. Facilities study: not mentioned

The Board approved $100,000 from capital reserves for a comprehensive facilities study on February 25, 2026, two weeks after this presentation. The RFQ scopes it as a 10-Year Long-Range Master Plan covering all eight RSU 5 locations, with demographic projections, alternative configurations, transportation logistics, and community engagement. Results are expected by December 2026. Converting PES is irreversible; the withdrawal process, by contrast, is fully reversible until the final referendum vote (Omnibus 6.2). The argument is not that no decisions should be made before the study, but that irreversible decisions should wait for professional data. Starting the withdrawal process preserves Pownal's options; converting PES eliminates them. (Omnibus 1.6)

### 10. "Magnetize" language

The presentation suggests PES could "magnetize" as an early childhood center, drawing families from outside the district. This is aspirational with no supporting analysis, enrollment projections, or market study. It also contradicts the presentation's own community identity lens: if community identity is a primary consideration, converting a 200-year-old neighborhood school into a magnet facility for non-residents is a significant departure from that value.

## What Is Conspicuously Absent

The presentation contains no:
- Dollar figures for any scenario
- Transportation cost estimates
- Per-town assessment impact analysis
- Enrollment trend data (PES is the only elementary with consistent growth: 89 to 105)
- PES staffing data (leanest school in the district, -28.4% FTE over 9 years)
- Reference to the $100K facilities study (approved two weeks after the presentation)
- Distinction between EC SpEd mandate (~25 IEP students) and universal PreK (128 students)
- Cost-sharing formula analysis or tax equity data
- RSU 5's 17-year history of restructuring proposals affecting Pownal
- PES educational outcomes (GreatSchools 8/10, 9:1 student-teacher ratio, top-10 elementary in Maine)

## Select Board Letter (Pending Approval, March 23, 2026)

Also posted to the March 23 agenda is a draft letter from the Pownal Select Board (signed by Chair Jon Morris, Katherine Day, and George Pottle) to Superintendent Gray. The letter is on the agenda for approval; it has not yet been sent. Key elements:

- **Contests the "repurposing" characterization.** The Town asserts the action should be classified as "school closing," not "school repurposing." This distinction has statutory implications under §1512.
- **Initiates FOAA requests** for financial data needed to evaluate withdrawal under §1466, including bonds, debt service, leases, property division, and monetary assets.
- **Demonstrates institutional engagement.** The Select Board is formally engaged in the withdrawal evaluation process. This is a town-level action, not solely a parent advocacy effort.
- **Cites specific withdrawal statutes** (§1466(4)(A)(6), (7), (10)), indicating legal preparation.

## The Engagement Contradiction

The presentation centers "community engagement" and "community identity" as primary decision lenses. Slide 8 ("Importance of Community Identity") states: "Each school is an expression of community identity and history." Slide 16 asks: "How shall we engage the community, ALL communities?" The closing slide declares: "Each Scenario Requires Community Engagement."

The historical record tells a different story.

The Omnibus (Sections 7.3, 7.4, 8.1-8.4) documents a 17-year pattern in which Pownal mobilizes in unified opposition and the Board proceeds regardless:

- **2010:** Pownal's middle school grades closed, moved to FMS. Proceeded over Pownal opposition.
- **2015-present:** Staffing reductions disproportionately affect PES. The school lost 28.4% of FTE, the largest decline of any school, including the elimination of dedicated ELA specialist, Math specialist, and Teacher Support Team positions.
- **2019, 2023:** The Finance Committee recommended cost-sharing formula reform. Both times, the recommendation was not adopted by the Board.
- **February 25, 2026:** 17 Pownal residents spoke during public comment on the reconfiguration proposal, the largest Pownal turnout in the entire 17-year minutes archive. The Board approved the facilities study but did not pause or modify the restructuring conversation.

This pattern is the core reason Pownal has concluded that protections within RSU 5 cannot be relied upon. The presentation advocates for community involvement and shared decision-making, but the documented record shows that Pownal's unified input has not altered a Board outcome in 17 years of operation.

This observation belongs in internal strategy documents. In public materials, the data speaks for itself: the Omnibus (Sections 7.3, 7.4) presents the historical pattern factually and lets readers draw their own conclusions.

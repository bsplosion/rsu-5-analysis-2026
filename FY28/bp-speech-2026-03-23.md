# Select Board Meeting Speech — Monday 3/23/2026 (v2)

**Target: 5 minutes (~750 words)**

---

Good evening. My name is Brandon Perry, 290 Fickett Rd. I'm a Pownal parent with two children entering PES, and a member of Save PES, a group of parents, educators, and community members you'll be hearing from tonight.

Superintendent Gray, thank you for coming to Pownal tonight. Other speakers will have questions for you directly. I want to use my time to share what our independent analysis found.

You just heard the Superintendent describe the pressures this district faces: rising health insurance, salary obligations, special education costs. We agree. Those pressures are real, statewide, and RSU 5 is not a spending outlier. We built an independent analysis using 70+ public sources: district budgets, tax records, state education filings. All of it is cited, verifiable, and available to anyone who wants to check our work.

Where we disagree is with the proposed scenarios, which contain no financial modeling: no cost estimates, no transportation analysis, no per-town impact. So we built one.

Two scenarios affect PES. One converts it to a centralized preschool center. The other makes it a district-wide 6th grade school. Both mean Pownal loses its K-5 elementary and our children bus to Durham. When you run the costs, the preschool scenario produces a net cost of $87,000 to $161,000 per year after absorbing 105 students at other schools and running 8 new bus routes. It does not save money. The 6th grade scenario does not address the early childhood mandate at all.

The mandate requires special education services for roughly 25 preschool-age children with disabilities. It does not require consolidating all 128 PreK students from three towns into one building. That is a policy choice, not a legal requirement.

[beat]

Alternatives exist. Consolidating middle school grades across existing buildings saves $479,000 to $804,000 per year while keeping every community school open. Redrawing attendance boundaries to send students to whichever school is closest would bring PES enrollment to 120 and its cost per student to the district average. These changes lower assessments for all three towns.

RSU 5 ranks in the top 10% of Maine districts, and PES is part of that. It operates on the lowest budget of any school in the district, the slowest budget growth, and is the only elementary with consistent enrollment growth, up from 89 to 105 students over three years.

[slow down]

We would prefer to resolve this within RSU 5. Here is where I need to be direct. The Board approved a $100,000 professional facilities study in February, with results due in December. Converting a school is irreversible. That data should be driving this decision. We hope it will.

[pause]

But we cannot plan around that hope. We have examined every available mechanism for protecting PES within the district. Reorganization plan amendments. Interlocal agreements. Legislative action. Even the existing statute that is supposed to require a town vote before closing a school. That statute has never been tested in court for a conversion like this, and even if it applies, it only blocks one attempt at a time. 

None of these mechanisms can be completed before PES is at risk, and none can prevent a conversion if the Board decides to proceed. Every one depends on specific Board actions that we cannot compel.

[beat]

Maine law gives us another path: withdrawal. Forty-three towns across fifteen districts have used it successfully since 2012. It gives Pownal voters the final say through referendum. Under state law, the final referendum must occur by November, before the facilities study even reports. That is not the timeline we would choose. It is the timeline the statute requires, and it is why starting now is not optional.

Independence means Pownal operates PES as a K-5 school and tuitions secondary students to Freeport, the same schools they attend today. After one-time transition costs in Year 1, the ongoing range runs from meaningful savings to roughly $124 per household more than we pay today. The range crosses break-even. Every cost line, high and low, is published in our analysis so there are no surprises.

This is not a separation from our neighbors. Pownal students continue at Freeport's middle and high schools. That tuition revenue stays with the district. Our kids will still play on the same teams, our families will still volunteer at the same events, and we will still show up for the district-wide improvements that benefit everyone's children.

Tonight we are circulating a withdrawal petition, and we are asking you to sign it. The process has built-in checkpoints: a town authorization vote, state review, and a final referendum where every Pownal voter decides. Every scenario presented tonight is either status quo, which the Board itself has called untenable, or conversion of PES. That means PES is on the table. Pownal has been through this before. After our middle school was closed, we were assured PES would remain. Assurances are not protections.

[beat]

If Pownal has not completed withdrawal before the Board acts, there is no legal remedy available. The full process must finish by November. We need your support tonight.

For seventeen years, Pownal has raised concerns about transparency, about equitable cost-sharing, about decisions made without the input of affected communities. Tonight, we can change that. Not with anger, not by walking away from our neighbors, but by taking responsibility for the school our community built, and by staying engaged in the improvements that benefit every family in this district. Sign the petition, talk to your neighbors, and come to the authorization vote. Let's end seventeen years of uncertainty and build something better for all of our communities. Thank you.

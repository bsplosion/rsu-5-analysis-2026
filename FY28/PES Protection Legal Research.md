## 1. Existing Statutory Protections

Maine law already provides a two-layer protection against school closure in RSUs. Understanding the strength and limits of these protections is essential before evaluating additional mechanisms.

### §1511: Supermajority Board Vote

**Full text (Title 20-A §1511):**

> A school operated within the regional school unit may not be closed for lack of need unless closure of the school is approved at a regular or special meeting of the regional school unit board by an affirmative vote of 2/3 of the elected membership or voting power of those serving on the regional school unit board at the time of the vote.

**Application to RSU 5:** The RSU 5 Board has 11 elected members with weighted voting (Freeport: 6 members/590 votes, Durham: 3/295, Pownal: 2/115). A 2/3 supermajority of total voting power requires approximately 667 of 1,000 total votes. This means all Freeport members (590) plus at least 77 votes from Durham or Pownal members would need to vote for closure. Pownal's 2 members (115 votes) alone cannot block closure, but it requires a broad coalition.

**Strength:** Moderate. The 2/3 threshold is higher than a simple majority but is achievable if Freeport and Durham members align.

**Citation:** PL 2007, c. 240, Pt. XXXX, §13; amended PL 2011, c. 171, §4; PL 2011, c. 678, Pt. K, §1.

### §1512: Municipal Referendum

**Full text (Title 20-A §1512):**

> A school in a member municipality of a regional school unit may not be closed unless the voters in the member municipality vote on the article in accordance with the referendum procedure set forth in this chapter.

The referendum ballot must read:

> "Do you favor authorizing the board of directors of [RSU 5] to close [Pownal Elementary School]? The additional cost of keeping the school open has been estimated by the regional school unit board to be $___."

**Key provisions:**

- **Who votes:** Only voters in the host municipality (Pownal), not the entire RSU. (§1512(1))
- **Cost of election:** Borne by the RSU, not the municipality. (§1512(1))
- **If voters reject closure:** The school stays open, but Pownal becomes liable for "the amount that would be saved if the school were closed." The Commissioner must approve the cost determination. (§1512(2))
- **Subsequent years:** If the Board votes to close again in a later year and voters again keep it open, the municipality remains liable for additional costs. If the Board votes to close and voters do not affirmatively keep it open, the school closes. (§1512(3))
- **Definition of closing:** "Any action by the regional school unit board that has the effect of providing no instruction for any students at that school." (§1512(4))

**Strength:** Strong but not permanent. Pownal voters have veto power over each closure attempt, but:
1. The financial penalty for exercising the veto could be substantial.
2. The Board can re-initiate closure proceedings year after year.
3. The definition of "closing" may not cover all scenarios (see Section 3).

**Critical financial detail for RSU 5:** Under the Omnibus Analysis (Section 2.3), the Superintendent's Scenario 2 produces a net annual cost of approximately $161,000 after accounting for absorption and transportation costs. If the "savings" from closure are negative, the §1512 financial penalty to Pownal could be zero or negligible. This significantly strengthens Pownal's §1512 position.

**Citation:** PL 2007, c. 240, Pt. XXXX, §13; amended PL 2007, c. 668, §25; PL 2009, c. 213, Pt. OOOO, §1.

## 2. RSU Reorganization Plan Amendments

### What the Reorganization Plan Is

The RSU's Reorganization Plan is its founding document, approved by voters in each member municipality and by the Commissioner of Education. For RSU 5, this was created through an 18-month planning process involving Durham, Freeport, and Pownal representatives. The plan was approved by the state and voters of each community. (Source: RSU 5 RPC Historical page)

### What the Plan Must Include

Under §1461(3)(A), the plan must address:

1. Units of school administration to be included
2. Size, composition, and apportionment of the governing body
3. Method of voting of the governing body
4. Composition, powers, and duties of local school committees
5. Disposition of real and personal school property
6. Disposition of existing school indebtedness
7. Assignment of personnel contracts and collective bargaining agreements
8. Disposition of existing school funds and financial obligations
9. Transition plan for budget development
10. Documentation of public meetings
11. Explanation of how units will proceed if some fail to approve
12. Estimate of cost savings
13. **"Such other matters as the governing bodies of the school administrative units in existence on the effective date of this chapter may determine to be necessary"** (§1461(3)(A)(13))

### The "Such Other Matters" Clause

Clause (13) is the key opening. It permits the reorganization plan to include matters beyond the enumerated list, as long as the governing bodies determine them "necessary." This is the statutory basis for arguing that a school protection provision could be added to the Reorganization Plan.

### Can the Plan Be Amended?

**The statute does not explicitly address post-formation amendments to the Reorganization Plan.** This is a significant gap in the law. However, several principles apply:

1. **The plan was approved by voters in each municipality.** Any amendment that alters the terms approved by voters would logically require the same voter approval process. The plan is not a board policy that can be changed by board vote; it is a voter-approved foundational document.

2. **§1502 provides the referendum mechanism.** RSU referendums are initiated by warrant prepared and signed by a majority of the RSU board directors, with public hearing at least 7 days before the vote.

3. **Precedent from Mount Desert Island (2024-25):** The Mount Desert Island Regional School System revised its reorganization plan and brought the revised version to voters in a June referendum, demonstrating that reorganization plans can be amended through voter referendum.

4. **§1481-A(3)(B) expressly requires it for cost-sharing:** "A process of amending the cost-sharing formula must be included in the reorganization plan." This confirms the legislature contemplated amendments to at least some provisions of the plan.

### Could a School Protection Provision Be Added?

**Theoretically yes, through the following process:**

1. The RSU 5 Board drafts an amendment to the Reorganization Plan adding a provision such as: *"No school operating in any member municipality as of the date of this amendment may be closed unless voters in that municipality approve the closure by referendum, and no grade reconfiguration that has the effect of eliminating K-5 instruction at any school shall be undertaken without the same approval."*

2. The Board votes to submit the amendment to referendum under §1502.

3. Voters in each municipality vote on the amendment.

4. The Commissioner reviews and approves (or recommends changes to) the amended plan.

**Challenges:**

- **Board initiation required.** Under §1502, the referendum warrant must be "prepared and signed by a majority of the regional school unit board directors." Pownal cannot unilaterally force an amendment referendum. The Board would need to agree to put it on the ballot.
- **No guarantee of passage.** Even if the Board puts the amendment on the ballot, voters in all three municipalities must approve. However, this protects all schools (not just PES), which may make it politically viable.
- **Untested enforceability.** No Maine court has ruled on whether a reorganization plan provision can permanently constrain future board action on school closure. A future board might argue the provision conflicts with its statutory authority under §1476 (powers and duties) or that §1511/§1512 already provides the exclusive framework for school closure decisions.
- **Commissioner approval uncertain.** The Commissioner must find the plan "meets the requirements of this chapter" (§1461(4)). A school protection provision is not among the enumerated requirements, but the catch-all clause may support it.

### Voter Approval Requirements

Based on the statutory framework and precedent:

- An amendment to the Reorganization Plan would require voter approval in each member municipality via referendum.
- The referendum process under §1502 requires 30 days' notice, public hearing, and a warrant signed by a majority of the Board.
- Each municipality votes separately; the plan is approved by majority vote in each.

## 3. The Reconfiguration Loophole

### The Problem

§1512(4) defines "school closing" as "any action by the regional school unit board that has the effect of providing no instruction for any students at that school." If the Board converts PES to an Early Childhood center under Scenario 2, PreK students would continue receiving instruction at the building. Arguably, this is not a "closure" under the statutory definition because instruction continues for some students.

### MSAD #1 Precedent (2026)

In February 2026, MSAD #1 in Presque Isle converted Pine Street Elementary from a full elementary to PreK-Grade 1 as a "grade reconfiguration" while separately processing the closure of Zippel Elementary through the referendum process. This precedent suggests Maine school districts draw a distinction between:
- **Reconfiguration** (changing grade levels served, some instruction continues) = administrative decision
- **Closure** (eliminating all instruction) = requires referendum

### The Superintendent's View

At the Durham Select Board meeting on March 10, 2026, Superintendent Gray stated he believes the grade changes at PES would constitute a school closure requiring a town vote from Pownal. This is the administrator's stated belief, not a legal ruling, but it would be difficult for the same superintendent to later reverse this position.

### Counterarguments Available to Pownal

1. **Purposive interpretation:** §1512 protects "a school," not "a building." PES as a K-5 elementary school ceases to exist under Scenario 2. The legislature's purpose was to protect communities from losing their school, not to permit closure-by-relabeling.

2. **De facto closure:** If all K-5 students are transferred out of PES, the practical effect on the community is identical to a closure. Courts applying statutory interpretation principles would look at the substance, not the label.

3. **Legislative intent:** §1512 was enacted as part of the 2007 RSU consolidation law specifically to protect communities worried about losing their schools through consolidation. Allowing reconfiguration to circumvent the protection would defeat the legislative purpose.

4. **No court ruling exists.** This is an open question in Maine law. No appellate court has addressed whether grade reconfiguration constitutes closure under §1512(4).

### How a Reorganization Plan Amendment Would Close This Loophole

A well-drafted plan amendment could define "closure" more broadly than the statute:

> *For purposes of this Reorganization Plan, "school closure" includes any action that eliminates K-5 instruction at any school operating in any member municipality, regardless of whether other instruction (including early childhood or special education programming) continues at the facility.*

This definition, if embedded in the voter-approved Reorganization Plan, would close the reconfiguration loophole by making it clear that removing K-5 grades from PES triggers the same protections as a full closure.

## 4. Cost-Sharing Formula Protections (§1481-A)

### Current Framework

RSU 5's cost-sharing follows §1481-A, which permits cost-sharing methods included in the reorganization plan. The statute allows three approaches:

1. **Property valuation method** — costs shared proportional to each municipality's property fiscal capacity
2. **Pupil-based method** — based on number of resident pupils
3. **Hybrid or other methods** — any combination, or any other factor (§1481-A(3)(A)(2)(d))

### Amendment Process

§1481-A(3)(B) explicitly requires: "A process of amending the cost-sharing formula must be included in the reorganization plan." This means the RSU 5 Reorganization Plan already contains (or should contain) a defined process for amending the cost-sharing formula.

### Could Cost-Sharing Be Tied to School Protection?

**Theoretically possible under §1481-A(3)(A)(2)(d)**, which allows cost-sharing based on "any other factor or combination of factors." A formula amendment could include provisions such as:

- An automatic cost-sharing adjustment triggered by school closure that shifts the financial burden of closure to the towns that voted for it
- A per-school operational floor (e.g., "the cost-sharing formula shall allocate sufficient funds to operate at least one elementary school in each member municipality")
- An explicit link between cost-sharing and school preservation

**Practical limitations:** The Finance Committee has recommended cost-sharing reform four times and been rejected each time. Any formula change requires political support from Freeport and Durham representatives on the Board. However, formula reform combined with other savings (as shown in the Omnibus Analysis Path C) could produce net assessment decreases for all three towns, making it politically viable.

### The "Municipality May Raise Money" Provision

The final paragraph of §1481-A states:

> Notwithstanding any provisions of law to the contrary, a municipality within a regional school unit may raise money and direct the spending of the funds to any school within the regional school unit.

This means Pownal could independently raise and direct funds to PES, supplementing RSU funding. This doesn't prevent closure but provides a mechanism for Pownal to cover any "additional costs" imposed under §1512 if voters reject a closure attempt.

## 5. Interlocal Agreements (30-A §2203)

### Legal Framework

Maine Title 30-A, Chapter 115 authorizes interlocal cooperation agreements between "public agencies," which includes both municipalities and school administrative units. The statute permits joint exercise of "any power or powers, privileges or authority exercised or capable of exercise" by the parties.

### Requirements

Under §2203(2), an interlocal agreement must specify:
- Duration
- Organization, composition, and nature of any entity created
- Purpose
- Financing method and budget
- Termination procedures and property disposition
- Any other necessary and proper matters

### Filing and Enforcement

- Agreements must be filed with the clerk of each municipality and the Secretary of State (§2204)
- Actions are maintainable against any party "whose default, failure of performance or other conduct caused or contributed to the incurring of damage or liability by the other parties jointly" (§2203(5))
- The agreement must be approved by ordinance, resolution, or other legal action of the governing bodies (§2203(1))

### Could Pownal Negotiate a School Protection Agreement?

**The legal framework supports it, with significant caveats:**

1. **Both parties must agree.** An interlocal agreement requires the RSU 5 Board to be a willing party. Pownal cannot impose terms unilaterally.

2. **Scope of authority.** §2203(8)(B) provides: "No essential legislative powers, taxing authority or eminent domain power may be delegated by agreement to a joint authority or administrative entity." The power to close a school is arguably an exercise of the Board's statutory authority under §1476, not a "legislative" power. But the Board could voluntarily constrain its own exercise of that authority through contract.

3. **Enforceability.** The agreement would be a binding contract between public entities, enforceable in court under §2203(5). If the RSU subsequently voted to close PES in violation of the agreement, Pownal could seek injunctive relief.

4. **Binding on successors.** The agreement's termination provisions would control whether it survives changes in Board composition. A well-drafted agreement would include: (a) a specific duration (e.g., 10 years with automatic renewal), (b) termination only by mutual consent plus voter referendum, and (c) explicit binding on successor boards. The Freeport withdrawal agreement provides precedent: it was "binding on any successor SAU."

5. **Tension with statutory scheme.** A court might find that an interlocal agreement restricting the Board's §1511/§1512 closure authority conflicts with the statutory framework, which already defines the closure process. However, the agreement could be structured not as a restriction on authority but as a voluntarily assumed contractual obligation.

### Model Agreement Provisions

A Pownal-RSU 5 interlocal agreement could include:

- **School operation commitment:** RSU 5 commits to operating at least one K-5 elementary school in each member municipality for the duration of the agreement
- **Definition of "operation":** Providing K-5 instruction with enrollment capacity for all resident students in grades K-5
- **Duration:** 10 years, automatically renewing in 5-year terms unless terminated
- **Termination:** Only by mutual written agreement of both parties, approved by referendum in each affected municipality
- **Remedy for breach:** Automatic authorization for the affected municipality to begin §1466 withdrawal proceedings; RSU liable for municipality's withdrawal process costs
- **Cost-sharing provisions:** Linked to maintaining school operations (see Section 4)

## 6. Board Policy vs. Bylaw vs. Reorganization Plan

### Hierarchy of Durability

| Level | How Changed | Durability | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **State statute** | Legislature only | Highest | §1511, §1512, §1466 |
| **Private & special law** | Legislature only | Very high | PS Law Ch. 19 (SAD 12 exemption) |
| **Reorganization Plan** | Voter referendum (§1502) | High | RSU formation terms, cost-sharing formula, board composition |
| **Interlocal agreement** | Mutual consent of parties | Moderate-high | 30-A §2203 agreements; contract law applies |
| **Board bylaws** | Board vote (typically supermajority) | Moderate | Meeting procedures, voting rules, committee structure |
| **Board policy** | Board vote (simple majority) | Low | Operational policies, curriculum, staffing |

### Why Board Policy Is Insufficient

A Board policy stating "PES shall not be closed" can be rescinded by a simple majority vote of the same or any future board at any regular meeting. There is no legal barrier to a future board reversing such a policy. **Board policies cannot bind future boards.**

### Bylaws: Slightly Better but Still Limited

Board bylaws typically require a supermajority or advance notice to amend (e.g., RSU 1's bylaws require amendments to be proposed at one meeting and voted on at the next). However:
- Bylaws cannot impose requirements beyond what state law permits
- Bylaws cannot raise the school closure threshold above the statutory 2/3 already required by §1511
- A determined board majority can amend bylaws through a two-meeting process

### The Reorganization Plan: The Strongest Local Mechanism

Because the Reorganization Plan was approved by voters in each municipality:
- It cannot be changed by Board action alone
- Amendment requires a referendum process (§1502)
- It functions as a quasi-constitutional document for the RSU
- Provisions embedded in the plan have the strongest claim to permanence of any locally-controllable mechanism

## 7. Private and Special Laws

### What They Are

The Maine Legislature can pass "private and special laws" that apply to specific entities. These are acts of the legislature that create rules for a named school district, municipality, or other entity.

### Precedent: PS Law Chapter 19 (124th Legislature, 2009)

The Legislature exempted three specific school units (SAD 12, School Union 37, School Union 60) from the consolidation mandates of the 2007 RSU law. The law directed the Commissioner to treat these geographically isolated districts as exceptions. This demonstrates the Legislature's willingness to enact district-specific protections when circumstances warrant.

### Could Pownal Seek a Private and Special Law?

**Yes, theoretically.** Pownal could petition its state legislators to introduce a bill providing that:

> *"Notwithstanding any provision of Title 20-A to the contrary, Pownal Elementary School, located in the Town of Pownal and operated within Regional School Unit No. 5, may not be closed or have its K-5 instructional program eliminated without the approval of the voters of the Town of Pownal by referendum."*

This would:
- Have the force of state law (highest durability)
- Cannot be overridden by board action
- Close the reconfiguration loophole by specifically referencing K-5 instruction
- Require no agreement from the RSU 5 Board

**Practical challenges:**
- Requires a willing state legislator to sponsor the bill
- Must pass both chambers and be signed by the Governor
- Could be seen as legislative micromanagement of local school governance
- May face opposition from education committee members concerned about precedent
- The Legislature could repeal or amend it in any future session

### This Is Probably the Most Powerful Available Tool

A private and special law is the only mechanism that creates protection without requiring RSU 5 Board cooperation. It operates at the same level as the statutes the Board relies on for its authority. If the political will exists, this is the most durable protection available.

## 8. Constitutional and Statutory Rights

### Maine Constitutional Right to Education

Maine's constitution commits to providing all school-aged persons "an opportunity to receive the benefits of a free public education." This right is enforced through local school administrative units. However:

- The constitution guarantees access to public education, not access to a specific school or building
- No Maine court has held that closing a school violates the constitutional right to education, as long as students are reassigned to other schools
- *School Administrative District No. 1 v. Commissioner of Education* (1995) upheld the state's funding formula against equal protection challenges, affirming broad legislative discretion in structuring education delivery

### Federal Equal Protection

*Eulitt v. Maine Department of Education* (2004, 1st Circuit) addressed tuition payment to sectarian schools and is not directly relevant to school closure. No federal court has recognized a constitutional right to a specific local school.

### No Constitutional Right to a Local School

**Finding: Neither the Maine Constitution nor the U.S. Constitution provides a right to a specific local elementary school.** The constitutional guarantee is to education, not to the means of delivering it. A school board's decision to close a school and reassign students to another school does not violate constitutional rights, as long as students continue receiving education.

### Statutory Protections Are the Primary Shield

The real protections are statutory (§1511, §1512, §4102), not constitutional. This means the protections are only as durable as the statutes that create them. The Legislature could theoretically amend or repeal §1512 in any session, though this is politically unlikely given the strong rural constituency that relies on it.

## 9. Precedent from Maine Communities

### SAD 75 / Harpswell (2026)

SAD 75 considered 12 consolidation options and eliminated all that would leave any of its four member towns without a school. After community forums, architect Lisa Sawin noted "a really strong desire to have a school in each community." The district chose renovation over closure. **Lesson:** Strong community engagement can prevent closure, but this was a board decision, not a binding legal protection. A future board could revisit the question.

### MSAD #1 / Presque Isle (2026)

Converted Pine Street Elementary from full elementary to PreK-Grade 1 as a "grade reconfiguration" while separately closing Zippel Elementary through referendum. **Lesson:** Grade reconfiguration is being used in Maine to restructure schools without triggering closure referendum requirements. This is the precedent that creates the §1512 loophole concern for Pownal.

### Freeport / RSU 5 (2013-2014)

Freeport voted 953-768 to begin RSU 5 withdrawal. A full withdrawal agreement was negotiated containing education continuity provisions (student enrollment guarantees, tuition rates, transportation, property transfer, staff rights). The agreement was explicitly "binding on any successor SAU" and could "only be terminated by mutual written agreement with Commissioner approval." Final referendum failed by 76 votes (2,152-2,228). **Lesson:** The Freeport agreement demonstrates that binding provisions with successor clauses can be negotiated within the RSU framework. Similar language could be used proactively in an interlocal agreement or reorganization plan amendment.

### RSU 4 / Sabattus (2024)

Voters approved closing Sabattus Primary School 329-151 after the Board's 2/3 supermajority vote. All three towns in the district voted. **Lesson:** When voters approve closure, the §1512 process works as designed. Protection depends on sustained community opposition.

### Embden / RSU 74 (2025)

Currently pursuing withdrawal. Contributes nearly a third of RSU 74's local budget with less than a fifth of students (similar to Pownal's situation). Authorized $50,000 for the process. **Lesson:** Withdrawal remains a viable path for towns with disproportionate tax burdens.

### PS Law Chapter 19 Exemptions (2009)

Three small, geographically isolated school districts received legislative exemptions from consolidation mandates. **Lesson:** The Legislature has been willing to create district-specific protections when circumstances warrant.

## 10. Practical Mechanisms

### 10.1 Supermajority Requirements for Closure

**Can the threshold be raised above 2/3?**

State law sets the floor at 2/3 (§1511). The question is whether the RSU can set a higher threshold (e.g., 3/4 or 4/5).

- **In board bylaws:** Likely not enforceable beyond the statutory 2/3. Bylaws implement and supplement statutes; they cannot modify them.
- **In the Reorganization Plan:** More plausible. The plan was approved by voters and constitutes a higher-order document than bylaws. An amendment adding a 3/4 or 4/5 requirement for school closure could be argued as a condition of the plan's voter approval. However, no Maine court has addressed whether a reorganization plan can exceed statutory requirements.
- **By private and special law:** The Legislature could set any threshold it wishes for a specific district. This is the only guaranteed method.

### 10.2 Automatic Withdrawal Triggers

**Can the Reorganization Plan include an automatic withdrawal trigger?**

Example provision: *"If the Board votes to close or eliminate K-5 instruction at any school operating in a member municipality, the affected municipality is automatically authorized to begin the §1466 withdrawal process without the petition requirement."*

**Analysis:**

- §1466 prescribes a specific petition and vote process for withdrawal. An automatic trigger would bypass the 10% petition requirement and the initial authorization vote.
- The Commissioner, who must approve withdrawal agreements, might not accept a process that deviates from the statutory steps.
- However, a reorganization plan provision could commit the RSU to a specific position in withdrawal negotiations: *"If PES is closed, the RSU shall cooperate fully with Pownal's withdrawal under §1466, shall not contest the withdrawal, and shall agree to transfer PES property to Pownal upon withdrawal."*
- The plan could also appropriate funds for Pownal's withdrawal costs, eliminating the authorization vote as a barrier.

**More realistic approach:** Rather than automatic withdrawal, the plan could include a "school closure consequence" provision that makes closure financially unattractive:

- If a school is closed, the affected municipality's cost-sharing assessment decreases by the full amount of the closed school's operating costs
- The municipality retains the right to raise additional funds for school operations under §1481-A(final paragraph)
- The municipality receives an accelerated property transfer timeline

### 10.3 Enrollment-Based Protections

**Example provision:** *"No school may be closed or have its K-5 instruction eliminated if enrollment exceeds 75 students."*

**Analysis:**

- PES enrollment is currently ~105 and projected at 105 for FY27. This threshold would protect PES under current conditions.
- Could be included in the Reorganization Plan or an interlocal agreement.
- A board could manipulate enrollment by redistricting students away from PES before proposing closure. The provision should include anti-manipulation language: *"Enrollment shall be measured using the average of the three most recent October 1 counts, and any attendance zone changes affecting the school's enrollment require the same approval process as closure."*
- Creates a clear, objective standard rather than a subjective judgment about "need."

### 10.4 Financial Protections

**Example provision:** *"No school may be closed unless its per-student operating cost exceeds 150% of the district average for three consecutive years."*

**Analysis:**

- PES's per-student cost is currently ~$26,649 vs. district average ~$24,000 (111% of average). Under this threshold, PES would be far from the closure trigger.
- Creates a financial test that must be met before closure can even be considered.
- Should be combined with anti-manipulation provisions (e.g., the Board cannot strip programs from a school to artificially reduce its enrollment and increase its per-student cost).
- Could be embedded in the Reorganization Plan or the cost-sharing formula.

## 12. Recommended Strategy

Based on the legal research, a layered approach provides the strongest protection:

### Layer 1: Immediate (No Board Cooperation Needed)

- **Document the Superintendent's §1512 concession.** Superintendent Gray's March 10, 2026 statement that grade changes at PES would constitute a school closure requiring a Pownal vote is on the record. Ensure this is preserved in meeting minutes and public correspondence.
- **Prepare for §1512 defense.** If the Board attempts reconfiguration without referendum, Pownal should be prepared to seek injunctive relief. Legal counsel should prepare the purposive interpretation argument (Section 3).
- **Quantify the §1512 financial penalty.** If the Omnibus Analysis is correct that Scenario 2 produces net costs (not savings), the §1512 penalty to Pownal for vetoing closure may be zero. This makes the veto essentially free.

### Layer 2: Negotiated (Requires Board Cooperation)

- **Pursue a Reorganization Plan amendment** that:
  - Defines "school closure" broadly to include elimination of K-5 instruction
  - Requires voter approval in the host municipality for any such closure (enshrining §1512 protections in the plan itself)
  - Includes enrollment-based and/or financial thresholds that must be met before closure can be proposed
  - Applies to all schools in all municipalities (making it politically viable)

- **Alternatively or additionally, negotiate an interlocal agreement** under 30-A §2203 committing RSU 5 to maintain K-5 instruction in each municipality for a defined term, with breach remedies.

### Layer 3: Legislative (No Board Cooperation Needed)

- **Seek a private and special law** from the Maine Legislature specifically protecting PES's K-5 program, or more broadly protecting all schools in RSUs from closure-by-reconfiguration without voter approval.
- Coordinate with Pownal's state representative and senator.
- Frame as closing a loophole in §1512 that the MSAD #1 precedent has exposed.

### Layer 4: Withdrawal (No Board Cooperation Needed)

- **Initiate §1466 withdrawal.** This is the only mechanism that definitively protects PES by giving Pownal full control. The financial analysis suggests independence would save Pownal $390K-$617K annually. The withdrawal process takes 12-24 months and is achievable for FY28 (July 2027) with immediate action under an aggressive timeline, with FY29 (July 2028) as the more certain fallback.
- This is also the only protection mechanism that can both be completed within the timeline where PES is at risk and prevent conversion if the Board decides to proceed. Negotiated protections (Layers 2 and 3) provide a cause of action if breached, but cannot prevent conversion in real time — by the time courts resolve a dispute, PES would already be converted.

### Optimal Sequencing

1. **Now:** Document §1512 position; retain legal counsel; quantify §1512 penalty (Layer 1)
2. **Spring 2026:** Begin withdrawal petition process — this is the only mechanism that can definitively protect PES within the available timeline (Layer 4 initiation)
3. **Simultaneously:** Approach state legislators about a private and special law or §1512 amendment (Layer 3); propose Reorganization Plan amendment and/or interlocal agreement to the Board (Layer 2). These reforms benefit all three communities regardless of Pownal's membership status.
4. **If Layers 2-3 produce binding protections before the withdrawal referendum:** Pownal voters can evaluate whether those protections are sufficient when they vote
5. **If Layers 2-3 are incomplete or unenforceable:** Complete withdrawal process (Layer 4)

5. Superintendent'S REPORT:
A. State Subsidy Curtailment — Kelly Wentworth, Director of Finance, reported that on December
27, 2012 Governor LePage announced a 12.58 million reduction in general purpose aid to
schools for the current 2012-2013 school year. The anticipated impact to RSUS is estimated at
$138,193. The Administrative Team met to consider all areas of the budget with the goal of
saving positions and minimizing the impact to direct instruction of the students. Areas of true
recognized savings such as tuition costs, assessment fees to Region Ten Technical School, and
budgeted sporting teams that had low participation numbers and were not funded were
considered first. The Team worked collaboratively to come up with the following areas of
anticipated reductions and savings throughout the district:
Tuition/Assessment Savings $33,000
Contingency $10,093
Books/Equipment/Postage/Supplies $24,700
Professional Services $20,000
Stipends/Game Officials/Dues & Fees $13,400
Capital Projects/Reserves $10,000
Curriculum Materials $27,000
Total Anticipated Reductions/Savings $138,193
6. WORKSHOP:
A. FHS Conceptual Drawings and Budget/Committee Recommendation to the Board:
Michelle Broderick, a member of the Facilities Committee, started the presentation by
providing a timeline of work of the Committee as follows: spring of 2011 the Board of
Directors officially sanctioned the Committee; PDT Architects was hired for work on the
Master Plan; Committee meets monthly during the 2011-2012 School Year; informational
meetings were held in all three communities; June 2012 Master Plan presented to the Board;
June, 2012 the Board appropriated money for the Concept Design phase; Committee met twice
a month since September bringing us to tonight's presentation.

Lyndon Keck, PDT Architects, presented the Freeport High School Concept Design Report to
the Board. The Master Plan has a built in growth of 650 students at 80% capacity and can go up
to 780 working at 90%. The Plan identifies three major categories of deficiencies at the school:
Age ~ The original building, constructed in 1961, at 52 years old, needs upgrades,
renovations and replacement of components that are simply worn out.
Overcrowding — The building is undersized for its current enrollment of 540 students and
the building needs to be expanded to accommodate anticipated increased enrollment over
the next ten years.
Site Plan Deficiencies - Traffic congestion and pedestrian conflicts exist at the site and
athletic fields and facilities are insufficient to meet physical education and athletic
programs.
The recommendations in the Master Plan are as follows: replace the poorly constructed
Industrial Arts building in the northwest corner of the high school with a two-story, 31,000
square foot addition to allow for expanded classroom and programming needs to address
overcrowding while improving vehicular circulation on the site; upgrade finishes to the
classrooms, restrooms, and hallways in the 1961 building; provide a new vestibule with state-
of-the art security elements at the existing front entrance; renovate and expand the
administrative offices; upgrade the art, STEM, and computer science program classrooms and
move them to the location of the existing library; relocate the library and adjacent learning lab
to the location of the existing cafeteria and kitchen; construct a new kitchen, food court, and bus
drop off in the lower level of the two story addition; build a track and all weather synthetic turf
field at the location of the existing soccer field; expand the physical education/wellness
classroom spaces adjacent to the gym and food court; upgrade the fiber optic network; replace
aging building envelope components including a new insulated membrane roof and new energy
efficient windows for the 1961 building; improve parent drop-off and expand visitor parking;
design the two-story addition to carry the weight of a future third floor.
Student Committee Members Abby Gray and Travis Libsack reviewed the results from the
student survey. Kim LaMarre, Committee Chair summarized what was presented this evening.
The overall cost of the project is $16,950.00 and the Committee recommends that the project be
placed on a June 2013 ballot in the form of a single bond value.

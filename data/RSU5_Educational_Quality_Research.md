## 6. Chronic Absenteeism

### Maine ESSA Framework

**Definition:** A student is chronically absent if enrolled ≥10 days and absent ≥10% of days enrolled (both excused and unexcused).

**Performance Levels:**
| Level | Criteria |
|---|---|
| Excelling | All student groups ≤5% chronic absenteeism |
| Meeting | All student groups <10% |
| Developing | At least one group <10% |
| Emerging | All groups ≥10% |

**Statewide Trends (KIDS COUNT / Annie E. Casey Foundation):**
- Maine chronic absenteeism peaked at ~28.4% in 2022
- Improved to ~24.4% by 2025
- Economically disadvantaged students: 34.5% chronic absenteeism (2025)

**RSU 5 Specific Data:**
- Freeport experienced peak chronic absenteeism during 2021-22 (matching statewide post-pandemic trend)
- Specific RSU 5 rates not publicly available in scraped sources; must be accessed via Maine DOE ESSA Dashboard Tableau visualization

## 7. Research on Small School / Class Size Educational Outcomes

### 7a. Krueger (1999) — Project STAR Re-analysis

**Citation:** Krueger, A.B. (1999). "Experimental Estimates of Education Production Functions." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 114(2), 497-532.

**Study Design:** Re-analysis of Tennessee's Student/Teacher Achievement Ratio (STAR) experiment — the largest randomized experiment on class size ever conducted. Students were randomly assigned to small classes (13-17 students), regular classes (22-25 students), or regular classes with a teacher aide (K-3).

**Key Findings:**
- Students in small classes gained ~4 percentile points on standardized tests in the first year
- Advantage expanded by ~1 percentile point per year in subsequent years
- Benefits persisted after return to regular classes: students stayed **6-13 months ahead** of peers through at least 8th grade
- **Larger effects for minority students and free lunch recipients**
- Teacher aides had little measurable effect on achievement
- Attending small classes narrowed the black-white gap in college test-taking by **54%**

**Source:** https://hceconomics.uchicago.edu/sites/default/files/events/Krueger_1999_QJE_v114_n2.pdf

---

### 7b. Chetty, Friedman, & Hilger (2011) — Long-Term Effects

**Citation:** Chetty, R., Friedman, J.N., Hilger, N., Saez, E., Schanzenbach, D.W., & Yagan, D. (2011). "How Does Your Kindergarten Classroom Affect Your Earnings? Evidence from Project STAR." *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 126(4), 1593-1660.

**Study Design:** Linked Project STAR experimental data from 11,571 students to administrative and tax records to track long-term outcomes to age 27+.

**Key Findings:**
- Students assigned to small classes were significantly more likely to attend college
- Students in higher quality classrooms (K-3) had **higher earnings at age 27**
- More experienced kindergarten teachers led to higher adult earnings
- **Test score gains faded out** in later grades, but **non-cognitive gains persisted**
- Replacing a bottom-5% teacher with an average teacher: increases lifetime classroom income by **>$250,000**
- Class quality effects: higher college attendance, better adult outcomes even when test score effects fade

**Source:** https://dash.harvard.edu/handle/1/9639983 and https://ideas.repec.org/a/oup/qjecon/v126y2011i4p1593-1660.html

---

### 7c. Campbell Collaboration Systematic Review (2021)

**Citation:** Filges, T., Sonne-Schmidt, C.S., & Nielsen, B.C.V. (2018/2021). "Small class sizes for improving student achievement in primary and secondary schools: a systematic review." *Campbell Systematic Reviews*.

**Study Design:** Systematic review of 148 reports from 41 countries.

**Key Findings:**
- Small classes have **at best a small effect on reading achievement**
- **No significant effect on mathematics**
- The Project STAR experiment appears to be an **outlier** in the broader literature
- Publication bias exists among studies in top economics journals

**Source:** https://ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8428040/

---

### 7d. Modern Meta-Analysis (2023)

**Citation:** Class Size and Student Achievement: A Modern Meta-Analysis.

**Key Findings:**
- Analysis of 1,767 estimates from 62 studies
- Implied class size effect is **zero for most identification approaches** except STAR
- Publication bias detected in top economics journals

**Source:** https://ideas.repec.org/p/osf/metaar/ekrqs_v1.html

---

### 7e. Glass & Smith (1979) — Original Meta-Analysis

**Citation:** Glass, G.V. & Smith, M.L. (1979). "Meta-Analysis of Research on Class Size and Achievement." *Educational Evaluation and Policy Analysis*, 1(1), 2-16.

**Key Findings:**
- One of the earliest meta-analyses on class size
- Found benefits of reduced class size, particularly below 15-20 students
- Foundational work that informed Project STAR design

**Source:** https://classsizematters.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/09/Glass-and-Smith.pdf

## 8. Maine School Consolidation Research

### 8a. LD 2007 — Maine's Consolidation Mandate (2007)

Maine enacted LD 2007 mandating reduction from 290 school districts to ~80. Districts required minimum 2,500 students (some exceptions at 1,200). Islands and tribal schools exempt. Implementation deadline: July 1, 2009.

**Projected Savings:** Gov. Baldacci claimed $241-250M over three years.
**Actual Outcome:** A Kansas State University researcher noted such plans "almost never" achieve predicted savings. Arkansas research on similar 2004 plan found no spending reduction or administrative size reduction.

**Source:** https://www.edweek.org/leadership/savings-from-school-consolidation-plans-uncertain/2007/03

---

### 8b. Trostel (2003) — Potential Efficiency Gains

**Citation:** Trostel, P.A. (2003). "Potential Efficiency Gains from Consolidation of Maine's Educational Resources." *Maine Policy Review*, 12(3).

**Key Findings:**
- Some economists argue smaller Maine schools may be too small to be cost-efficient
- Education in Maine costs more per student than national average
- But efficiency arguments are not conclusive

**Source:** https://digitalcommons.library.umaine.edu/mpr/vol12/iss3/11/

---

### 8c. Donis-Keller et al. (2013) — Post-Consolidation Study

**Citation:** Donis-Keller, C., O'Hara-Miklavic, B., & Fairman, J. (2013). "Improving Educational Opportunity and Equity through School District Consolidation." *Maine Policy Review*, 22(2).

**Study Design:** Examined 24 regional school districts one year after merger.

**Key Findings:**
- Districts made different choices when consolidating educational programs
- Varied outcomes and implementation strategies
- Success remained "open to debate" five years after the 2007 law

**Source:** https://digitalcommons.library.umaine.edu/mpr/vol22/iss2/7/

---

### 8d. School Size Choices — Maine Policy Review

**Citation:** "School Size Choices: Comparing Small and Large School Strengths." *Maine Policy Review*.

**Key Findings:**
- Small schools provide **greater personal attention**, more scheduling flexibility, stronger school-community connections
- Large schools offer broader course offerings and specialized programs
- School size alone explains **very little variation in 11th grade test scores**
- **No conclusive data that either small or large schools are consistently better**
- Recommendation: design schools meeting needs of particular students and communities

**Source:** https://digitalcommons.library.umaine.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?httpsredir=1&article=1244&context=mpr

## 9. Key Takeaways for RSU 5 Analysis

1. **Pownal Elementary is the top-performing school in RSU 5** — ranked #10 in all Maine elementary schools with a 96.07/100 overall score, the best reading proficiency (95%), and the lowest student-teacher ratio (9:1).

2. **All RSU 5 schools significantly outperform state averages** in both reading and math proficiency.

3. **Lower student-teacher ratios correlate with higher performance** within the district: Pownal (9:1 → #1 in district), Freeport MS (10:1 → #11 in state), vs. Freeport HS (13:1 → #18 in state).

4. **Pownal has the lowest economically disadvantaged rate** (12%) and the smallest enrollment (89), with 100% certified teachers.

5. **Research supports that class size effects are most impactful in early grades** (K-3), precisely where Pownal's 9:1 ratio operates.

6. **Long-term benefits of small class size persist even when test score gains fade** — Chetty et al. (2011) found higher college attendance and earnings.

7. **Maine consolidation research is inconclusive** — no definitive evidence that consolidating small, high-performing schools improves outcomes.

8. **RSU 5 has a notable racial discipline disparity** (2015-16 data): Black students 4.5x more likely to be suspended than White students.

9. **Chronic absenteeism data for RSU 5** is not readily available in public-facing web sources; the Maine DOE ESSA Dashboard (Tableau-based) must be accessed directly.
